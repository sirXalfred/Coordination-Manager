## Delegation & Redelegation

Source: Discussion - Yoroi improving their auto-delegate user flow has a severe impact on delegation stickiness, influencing whether users remain with their current DRep or passively switch.
Source: Discussion - With the DRep system we do have the "will of the people" because delegators can redelegate before a governance action is enacted after submission.
Source: Discussion - It benefits Cardano if ADA is exchanging hands and people often redelegate, as frequent redelegation signals active governance engagement.
Source: Discussion - Some DEXes redelegate voting power on behalf of their users, which may concentrate governance influence in protocol-level actors.
Source: Community feedback - The requirement to delegate to a DRep before withdrawing staking rewards creates perverse incentives and warps voting outcomes by pushing apathetic holders toward default or abstention options.
Source: Community feedback (@TheOCcryptobro) - Some wallets or platforms unfairly promote their own DReps, which undermines decentralized selection and skews delegation patterns.

## Accessibility & Participation

Source: Discussion - There are accessibility issues in Cardano governance: people need to know where to go and when to go to participate.
Source: Community feedback - Ongoing complaints exist about technical barriers, lack of user-friendly tools, wallet compatibility problems, and forced delegation mechanics leading to suboptimal governance engagement.
Source: Community feedback - Users highlight difficulties with wallets not fully supporting governance features, confusion around tools like GovTools, and short voting deadlines that limit participation.
Source: Community feedback (@maureenyam) - Reports of voting tools malfunctioning during key processes such as budget approvals have led to frustration over transparency and reliability.
Source: Discussion - We are used to having information from political parties, but DReps do not provide the same level of structured information, creating an information gap for delegators.

## Legitimacy & Democratic Principles - OSCE

Source: OSCE - Erosion of Legitimacy - The OSCE maintains that the "will of the people" is the only basis for the authority and legitimacy of a government. If participation drops significantly, the resulting decisions may no longer reflect the collective will, undermining the system's genuineness.
Source: OSCE - Budget constraints lead to a lack of genuine choice or an environment where rights cannot be exercised. The resulting government may lack a true public mandate.
Source: OSCE - When underfunding prevents political pluralism or creates chaotic conditions that observers cannot conduct an impartial assessment, the OSCE may determine that the entire election observation activity is "meaningless" and recommend against deployment.
Source: OSCE Election Observation Handbook - Election administration must be provided with funds "sufficient to meet its responsibilities" in a "transparent and timely manner." The OSCE views underfunded governance as a structural threat to the integrity, legitimacy, and equality of the democratic process.

## Market Effects on Governance

Source: Discussion - When ADA price drops and people move funds to centralized exchanges (CEXes), their governance participation is effectively removed from the on-chain system.
Source: Research methodology - Smart contract accounts and exchange-managed accounts must be filtered from governance analysis because tokens in these addresses are not controlled by any single user and cannot be used for governance. Including them would strongly distort results, especially voting power distribution.

## Structural & Systemic Issues

Source: Discussion - Political parties are elected for terms, whereas DReps do not have terms. However, a DRep going rogue risks their power position since delegators can redelegate at any time.
Source: Discussion - Compared to national governance, if a political party changes direction and people change their vote, it typically means something has gone very wrong. The DRep model differs because redelegation is fluid and continuous.
Source: Community feedback (medium.com) - Plutocratic elements exist where voting power is heavily skewed toward large ADA holders, making smaller holders feel disenfranchised or powerless in governance decisions.
Source: Community feedback - Calls exist for reforms such as making governance participation opt-in rather than forced, to improve genuine engagement and reduce noise from passive or coerced delegations.

## UX & Cognitive Load (Survey)

Source: Survey, DRep [R-002] - Concentration is driven by wallet defaults, creating a "sticky" influence that is hard to shift. Wallet UI/UX (Yoroi, Eternl) rewards visibility over merit.
Source: Survey [R-079] - Information and actions are spread across too many disparate platforms, creating cognitive overload and a "snob" bias that fragments the governance experience.
Source: Survey - Governance is currently "by developers, for developers." Participants report hours-long research sessions to perform simple voting tasks, leading to an effort-to-impact mismatch (cognitive fatigue).
Source: Survey - The requirement to interpret raw technical data such as CBOR hexes and CLI commands creates a "technocratic exclusion" barrier that locks out non-developers.
Source: Survey - Heavy concern from Japan and Africa regarding the lack of real-time translation for constitutional documents. English-centricity and legalese effectively disenfranchise non-English speaking regions.
Source: Survey - High security requirements (cold keys) are actively hindering Stake Pool Operator (SPO) participation in the voting process, creating a security vs. UX paradox.

## Power Dynamics & Accountability (Survey)

Source: Survey - Early-mover DReps benefit from a "power moat" created by wallet defaults that are rarely revisited by users, creating an incumbency advantage and a risk of a "permanent political class."
Source: Survey - A lack of DRep messaging tools or "performance scorecards" means DReps can vote against community interests with zero repercussions (accountability deficit).
Source: Survey - Delegators rarely reassess or change their delegation, allowing initial choices to calcify and perpetuate power concentration.
Source: Survey - DRep inactivity and poor communication are the primary triggers that cause delegators to actually change their delegation.
Source: Survey - Lack of DRep discovery and comparison tools perpetuates concentration by making it difficult for delegators to find and evaluate alternatives.
Source: Survey - There is consensus across all governance roles that power concentration is a systemic problem in Cardano governance.

## Incentives & Sustainability (Survey)

Source: Survey, CC Member [R-069] - Compensation is necessary for people to be able to fully devote themselves to governance. Lack of formal compensation means governance remains a "volunteer-only" activity, which is unsustainable.
Source: Survey - A polarizing debate exists between viewing governance as a professionalized role deserving compensation versus a "governance tax" on the treasury.
Source: Survey - Rationale publication by DReps is time-consuming and inconsistently rewarded, discouraging thorough engagement.
Source: Survey - The Constitutional Committee lacks sufficient resources and compensation to fulfill its mandate effectively.
Source: Survey - SPO governance participation is treated as low priority and is technically burdensome, reducing their involvement in voting.

## Constitutional & Infrastructure Integrity (Survey)

Source: Survey - The Constitution is viewed as too open to interpretation, particularly regarding "Net Change Limits" (NCL), creating constitutional ambiguity.
Source: Survey - Users describe "cobbling together" the truth from conflicting sources (GovTool vs. unofficial trackers), leading to disconnected infrastructure and information silos.
Source: Survey - Protocol logic requiring reward withdrawal to go through delegation forces "passive delegation" to default DReps just to access funds.
Source: Survey - Proposed solutions from respondents cluster around decentralization mechanisms as the primary path to fixing governance concentration.
